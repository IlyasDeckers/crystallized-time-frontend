Trails and decay. Right now flashes fade in their own cell. Instead, have each MIDI hit spawn a particle that drifts — pick a random direction at birth, store position as floats, advance position each frame, paint the cell at floor(position). At 2.5px per cell you'd see streaks of comet-tails crossing the grid. Add slight per-channel drift directions (channel 0 drifts up-right, channel 1 up-left, etc.) and you get 16 distinct migration patterns layered.
Channel as terrain. Each of the 16 channels owns a horizontal band of the viewport, or each pair owns a quadrant. Notes never appear at random — they appear in their channel's zone. Suddenly the visual encodes structure: you can see which channels are firing at a glance, polyrhythms become spatial.
Cellular automata fed by MIDI. Run Conway's Game of Life on the grid in the background — every frame, advance one generation. MIDI events seed live cells at random visible positions. The simulation organically shapes what MIDI plants. With the sparse rate of your substrate output, you'd get little gliders and oscillators emerging from each event, drifting and colliding. This pairs beautifully with the physics theme — emergent structure from local rules, perturbed by external input.
Reaction-diffusion (Gray-Scott). Two chemicals diffuse and react across the grid producing organic spots, stripes, and waves. MIDI events drop "chemical" at random cells. At 2.5px cells this would look like Petri-dish patterns evolving — extremely physics-coded, computationally cheap on a small viewport, and unlike anything generic on the internet. The substrate's outputs become disturbances in a continuous field rather than discrete pixels.
Persistent ghost layer. Don't fully clear the canvas each frame. Each frame, fill the whole canvas with rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.05) instead of clearRect. Everything you draw decays slowly. Flashes leave fading trails of themselves, motion leaves smears. Very analog-display feel. One-line change to the canvas grid; massively different look.
Pixel waves on clock pulses. When your /clock/pulse events arrive (chain A's master clock), spawn a ring of cells expanding outward from a center point. Tie the ring's center to the click position of the last click; tie ring color to the magnetization value carried in the message. The clock becomes a visible pulse radiating through space.
Magnetization as global tint. Sample chain A's /state/magnetization and use it as a global bias on the background — slight cool when negative, warm when positive, neutral at zero. The entire room "breathes" with the substrate's mean state. Subtle but constantly present. Pairs well with the discrete events on top.
Domain wall visualization. Walls are mobile objects with positions — render them literally. Each wall is a bright moving point, leaves a trail, blinks out when destroyed. The chain becomes a 1D landscape where you can see the walls dancing. Stack the chains as two horizontal bands (chain A above, chain B below) for the polyrhythmic case.
Phase portraits. Instead of (x, y) being grid coordinates, plot a 2D phase variable from the substrate. For example: x-axis = chain A magnetization, y-axis = chain B magnetization (or wall count, etc.). Each MIDI event drops a fading dot at the current point. Over time, you see the attractor — the actual shape of the substrate's dynamics in phase space. This is the kind of plot that's standard in physics papers but rarely animated; it would directly visualize "the substrate is locked" vs "the substrate is thermalizing" as different attractor geometries.
Per-cell decay variation. Currently every flash decays at the same rate. Give each cell a random "decay rate" generated from its grid coordinates. Some cells fade fast, some slow. The aggregate effect is a noise texture that's stable in place — flashes "stick" to certain regions of the plane more than others.
Cursor as gravity well for flashes. You already have the gravity warp on grid lines. Extend it to flash particles: flashes near the cursor get pulled in. Combined with the trails idea this is striking — the cursor becomes a literal attractor that pulls in MIDI activity.
